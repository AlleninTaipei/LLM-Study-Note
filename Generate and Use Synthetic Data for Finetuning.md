# Generate and Use Synthetic Data for Finetuning

[Source: Machine Learning for Synthetic Data Generation: A Review](https://arxiv.org/html/2302.04062v9#S1) **Synthetic data is generally defined as artificially annotated information generated by computer algorithms or simulations.**

The growing legal and ethical pressure around training data makes synthetic data an increasingly important strategy. Real-world incidents illustrate the stakes:

* [Google denies it copied ChatGPT to train its own AI chatbot Bard](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/google-denies-it-copied-chatgpt-to-train-its-own-ai-chatbot-bard/articleshow/99107700.cms?utm_source=contentofinterest&utm_medium=text&utm_campaign=cppst) — highlights IP concerns when using outputs from a competitor’s model as training data.
* [OpenAI suspends ByteDance’s account over ‘secretly using’ its AI tech](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/openai-suspends-bytedances-account-over-secretly-using-its-ai-tech/articleshow/106042266.cms?utm_source=contentofinterest&utm_medium=text&utm_campaign=cppst) — demonstrates that violating model terms of service during data generation carries real consequences.
* [Introducing Llama 3.1: Our most capable models to date](https://ai.meta.com/blog/meta-llama-3-1/) — Meta’s Llama 3.1 relied heavily on synthetically generated data to reach frontier-level performance, signaling that synthetic data is now mainstream even for frontier models.

Synthetic data has become critical for several reasons: real-world labeled data is scarce or expensive to collect, privacy regulations restrict the use of sensitive data (medical, legal, financial), and legal risks around scraping or distilling from proprietary models make controlled generation preferable. By generating data programmatically or via a capable teacher model, teams can create large, high-quality, and legally safer datasets on demand.

## Size of a dataset for fine-tuning a large language model (LLM)

When deciding the size of a dataset for fine-tuning a large language model (LLM), the major factors to consider are:

|Major factors|Consideration|
|-|-|
|Task Complexity|The complexity of the task or domain you are fine-tuning for affects the dataset size. More complex tasks or those requiring specialized knowledge typically need larger datasets to capture the required nuances.|
|Model Size|Larger models generally need more data to fine-tune effectively because they have more parameters to adjust. A smaller dataset may lead to underfitting, where the model doesn't learn enough to perform well on the task.|
|Overfitting Risk|Smaller datasets increase the risk of overfitting, where the model learns too much from the fine-tuning data and performs poorly on general or unseen examples. A larger dataset can help reduce this risk.|
|Training Resources|Larger datasets require more computational resources (time, memory, processing power) for fine-tuning. If resources are limited, it may be necessary to use a smaller dataset or optimize for more efficient methods like PEFT (e.g., LoRA).|
|Domain-Specific Requirements|For specialized tasks, even a small dataset may be effective if the task requires highly specific knowledge (e.g., legal documents, medical records). The dataset size should balance specificity and generalizability.|
|Diminishing Returns|At a certain point, increasing dataset size may yield diminishing returns, where additional data does not significantly improve performance. The size should be chosen to cover a wide enough variety without being excessively large.|

## General understanding in Domain-specific requirements

Example: Given the nature of the task, the model should handle a variety of customer queries related to motherboard specifications, troubleshooting, warranty, installation, etc. Here's a general guideline on dataset size for both 3B and 7B pre-trained models:

  * 3B Model (Smaller Model) Dataset Size: ~10K to 50K examples
    Reasoning: A 3B parameter model can perform well with a moderate dataset size, but its smaller capacity means it may need more focused data. Given the domain-specific nature (motherboard product queries), starting with 10K-50K carefully curated examples (covering common user issues, troubleshooting steps, and FAQs) would be sufficient to enable good generalization without overloading the model.
    Focus: Ensure that the dataset covers a wide range of motherboard-related questions, common problems, and specific answers related to product features.
  * 7B Model (Larger Model) Dataset Size: ~50K to 150K+ examples
    Reasoning: A 7B model has greater capacity and can benefit from a larger dataset to fully capture the nuances and details of product support queries. You might aim for 50K-150K+ examples, especially if the chatbot will handle diverse customer queries or more complex scenarios like in-depth technical troubleshooting or product comparison.
    Focus: A larger dataset should include more variety in phrasing, complex problem scenarios, and detailed explanations. You may also consider including multilingual support if the chatbot will cater to different regions.

> **Note**: These numbers are illustrative guidelines for a domain-specific customer support use case. Actual requirements vary significantly by task difficulty, data quality, and whether parameter-efficient methods (e.g., LoRA) are used — a high-quality 1K dataset can sometimes outperform a noisy 100K one.

## The process of collecting and annotating data

The process of collecting and annotating data is both time-consuming and expensive, leading to several challenges. Since machine learning is highly dependent on data, some of the key hurdles include:

  * Data quality: Ensuring high data quality is one of the most significant challenges faced by machine learning professionals. Poor-quality data can lead to incorrect or imprecise model predictions, causing confusion and misinterpretation.
  * Data scarcity: A substantial part of the current AI challenge arises from limited data availability. Either there are not enough datasets, or the cost of manual labeling is prohibitively high.
  * Data privacy and fairness: In many cases, datasets cannot be publicly shared due to privacy and fairness concerns. In such situations, generating synthetic data can be a viable solution.

## Self-Instruct

[Self-Instruct](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.10560) improves the instruction-following ability of a non-finetuned model (vanilla gpt-3) by bootstrapping off its own generations. First, they generate instructions, input context, and responses from the model. Then, they filter invalid or similar examples before using the remaining samples to finetune the original model.

## Distillation

Knowledge distillation is a technique that facilitates the transfer of knowledge from large, computationally expensive models to smaller, more efficient ones, while preserving accuracy. This enables deployment on less powerful hardware, resulting in faster and more efficient model evaluation.

* [Alpaca](https://crfm.stanford.edu/2023/03/13/alpaca.html) — Stanford's landmark work; fine-tuned LLaMA-7B on 52K instruction-following samples generated from GPT-3.5 (text-davinci-003), demonstrating that a small open model can follow instructions after cheap synthetic distillation.
* [Vicuna](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05685) — fine-tuned LLaMA on ~70K conversations collected from ShareGPT (real user interactions with ChatGPT), showing that user-shared chat logs are a powerful source of distillation data.
* [WizardLM](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.12244) — introduced **Evol-Instruct**, a method that automatically rewrites simple seed instructions into progressively harder variants (deeper, more complex, broader) using GPT-4, yielding richer training data without human effort.
* [Orca](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.02707) — Microsoft's model that distills *reasoning traces* (step-by-step explanations) from GPT-4, not just answers, enabling smaller models to learn *how* to reason rather than just *what* to answer.
* [Orca 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.11045) — extends Orca by training 7B/13B models to select the most appropriate reasoning strategy per task (step-by-step, recall, direct answer), improving calibration and safety.
* [WizardCoder](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.08568) — applies Evol-Instruct to code generation, evolving coding problems and solutions to train a stronger code-focused model.
* [Magicoder](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02120) — generates diverse coding problems by seeding GPT-4 with open-source code snippets, reducing the model's tendency to memorize common patterns.
* [WaveCoder](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.14187) — creates a balanced multi-task code instruction dataset covering generation, summarization, translation, and repair using a discriminator-generator pipeline.
* [Textbooks Are All You Need (Phi-1)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.11644) — Microsoft's Phi-1: shows that a 1.3B model trained on high-quality "textbook-like" synthetic Python code outperforms much larger models on coding benchmarks, emphasizing data quality over quantity.
* [Textbooks Are All You Need II (Phi-1.5)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.05463) — extends the textbook approach to general reasoning, producing a 1.3B model with common-sense and language understanding comparable to models 5× larger.
* [Starling-7B](https://starling.cs.berkeley.edu/) — trains a reward model on a large AI-feedback preference dataset (Nectar) and uses it for RLHF, demonstrating that synthetic preference labels can drive high-quality alignment.

## Risks and Quality Control

Using synthetic data introduces unique risks that must be actively managed.

### Model Collapse

When a model is repeatedly trained on its own outputs across multiple generations, errors and low-frequency patterns get amplified while diversity shrinks — a phenomenon called **model collapse** ([Shumailov et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.17493)). In practice this means:

* The model's output distribution narrows over successive self-improvement rounds.
* Rare but important knowledge can be "forgotten" because synthetic data over-represents high-probability outputs.
* Mitigation strategies include always mixing in a portion of real human-generated data, capping self-improvement iterations, and monitoring output diversity metrics between rounds.

### Quality Filtering

Not all generated samples are equal. Common filtering strategies before using synthetic data for training:

* **Deduplication** — remove near-duplicate samples using n-gram overlap (ROUGE) or embedding similarity to prevent the model from memorizing repeated patterns.
* **Reward model scoring** — score each candidate with a trained reward or quality model and keep only the top percentile; used extensively in RLHF pipelines.
* **Rule-based filtering** — discard samples that are too short, contain formatting artifacts, fail a language-detection check, or repeat the prompt verbatim.
* **Self-consistency filtering** — for reasoning tasks, generate multiple solutions and keep only examples where the majority of sampled answers agree (used in STaR and ReST).
* **Difficulty-aware selection** — prefer samples of moderate difficulty (not trivially easy or unsolvable) to maximize learning signal per training step.
